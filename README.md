# Lessons from skuas: Host ecology, tracking devices, and study design at the intersection of movement and disease ecology

## Sizemore, B., Burgueño, M., Streith, L., Van Tronder, A., Young, J. , McMahon, C., Clessin, A., White, J., Gamble, A.

Pathogens of wildlife are increasingly recognized as major threats to biodiversity, ecosystem stability, and human health, but the logistical difficulties associated with monitoring free-ranging wildlife has greatly hindered the study of these disease systems. Animal movement data presents itself as a means of assessing plausible transmission routes, particularly considering large, public repositories with datasets spanning taxa and locales. At the same time, little work has been done to assess how movement data collection methods that might lend themselves to disease risk assessment or preclude the utility of a dataset to such studies. We present a case study of such factors, particularly differences between two common tracking technologies and between capture methods, using data collected from skuas in the Falkland Islands/Islas Malvinas and the Antarctic Peninsula. We implement a Bayesian hidden Markov model to parse out “risky” behaviors for skuas, particularly foraging, which has been implicated in the spread of infectious diseases between seabird colonies. In Bayesian regression models, GPS loggers not only provide drastically lower estimates of skua home range size than Argos-PTT loggers but also documented higher variability in home range size, suggesting that selecting high resolution technologies may be better suited for describing heterogeneity in space use that affects pathogen spread. We also documented significant heteroskedasticity according to interactions between capture method and home island, with a particularly large effect size in the home range computed from foraging tracks. We recommend that researchers conducting movement-based disease studies consider: (1) the spatial and temporal resolution requirements for detecting transmission-relevant behaviors, and (2) potential interactions between host ecology and sampling methods. This work provides an evidence-based framework for evaluating movement dataset suitability for infectious disease research and highlights critical methodological considerations for future studies linking animal movement to pathogen transmission.
